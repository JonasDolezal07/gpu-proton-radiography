STRUCTURE OF THE RESULTS
-------------------------

Grid resolution degrades the radiograph significantly.  Correlation falls
monotonically from 1.0 (native) to 0.235 (coarsest tested), indicating
that field-grid resolution is a meaningful source of error — unlike the
timestep study, where the integrator was converged across the full practical
range.

Two distinct behaviours are visible:

1. Sharp degradation at coarse grids (dx > 6 mm)

   The two coarsest grids (4×4×8 and 8×8×16) produce heavily distorted
   radiographs (corr 0.23 and 0.58).  At 33 mm spacing the field is
   represented by only 4 points across the 100 mm field domain; at 14 mm
   spacing by 8 points.  The z-pinch caustic structure, which forms from
   field gradients on scales of order the pinch radius (~5–10 mm), is
   largely absent at these resolutions.

2. Near-plateau between 8×8×16 and 16×16×32

   The 8×8×16 and 16×16×32 grids produce nearly identical MSE
   (9.93×10⁻¹¹ vs 9.88×10⁻¹¹) and correlation (0.576 vs 0.599).
   Doubling the xy resolution from 8 to 16 cells gives essentially no
   improvement.

   Most likely explanation: the z-direction (128 → 16 or → 32 cells in the
   downsampled versions) is the resolution bottleneck at these grids.
   The native z-spacing is 0.63 mm vs the native xy-spacing of 1.59 mm —
   the field is sampled 2.5× more finely in z, suggesting the field has
   significant fine structure in z.  When z is downsampled from 128 to 16
   or 32 cells (z-spacing 5.3 mm and 2.6 mm respectively), this structure
   is lost regardless of the xy resolution.

   An anisotropic downsampling study (varying z-resolution independently of
   xy) would confirm this interpretation but is left for future work.

3. Significant residual error at 32×32×64 (dx = 3.2 mm, dz = 1.3 mm)

   Correlation is 0.792 at half the native resolution — a 21% gap from the
   reference.  This is large enough to matter for quantitative analysis.
   The radiograph morphology is broadly correct at this resolution but
   fine caustic structure is blurred.


CRITICAL LIMITATION: NO FINER REFERENCE AVAILABLE
---------------------------------------------------
This study can only confirm that coarser grids degrade the result relative
to the native 64×64×128 field.  It cannot confirm that the native grid is
itself sufficiently resolved.

To establish convergence of the native grid, one would need:

  Option A — Upsample with a known-good interpolation scheme
    Resample the native field to 128×128×256 using cubic interpolation
    and compare the native-resolution simulation to this finer version.
    Limitation: upsampling does not add information; it only tests
    whether the GPU's trilinear interpolation introduces error relative
    to a smoother (cubic) representation of the same data.

  Option B — Generate analytical test field at multiple resolutions
    Use a field with a known analytical form (e.g., infinite straight
    wire: B_phi = μ₀I/(2πr)) sampled at increasing resolutions.
    Compare the full-orbit radiograph to the analytical prediction at
    each resolution.  This is the most rigorous approach and the natural
    next step.

  Option C — Use MHD field data at multiple output resolutions
    If simulation output is available at multiple grid spacings from the
    same MHD run, the radiograph convergence with grid spacing can be
    assessed directly on physically realistic fields.

Until one of these is done, the correct statement is:

  "We demonstrate that halving the field-grid resolution from the native
  64×64×128 causes a 21% reduction in image correlation.  The native
  resolution was chosen to resolve field features on the ~2 mm scale of
  the pinch structure.  Convergence of the native grid itself is a topic
  for future work."


PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS
-----------------------
For the z-pinch field at native resolution (64×64×128, dx = 1.59 mm):

  — Resolution is not converged in the same sense as timestep or particle
    count.  There is no "plateau" above which adding resolution is free.

  — For external field inputs (e.g., GORGON MHD outputs, experimental
    reconstructions), the user should check that the field grid is fine
    enough to resolve the relevant structure.  A rule of thumb: at least
    5–10 grid cells across the minimum feature of interest.

  — The grid study also confirms the correctness of the GPU field-sampling
    code: as grid resolution increases the radiograph converges smoothly
    toward the native result, with no discontinuities or artefacts from
    the CLAMP_TO_EDGE boundary condition or the explicit domain-bounds
    check that returns zero outside the field volume.

  — Runtime is insensitive to grid resolution (all five runs ≈ 0.9 s)
    because the field is uploaded once and the total work is dominated by
    particle stepping, not field lookup.

The timestep and particle studies both showed the code operating in a
noise-limited regime where increasing resolution bought nothing.  The grid

This motivates the analytical-field convergence test (Option B above) as
